The Process

From your kitchen scraps to a neighbor's plate

Our program creates a simple loop: your organic waste becomes the soil that grows fresh food for Pittsburgh families who need it most.

1

You collect

Set aside vegetable peels, fruit scraps, coffee grounds, and eggshells. We provide a free collection bin when you sign up.

2

We pick up

Our team picks up your organic waste weekly from your doorstep — no extra effort from you.

3

We compost

Your scraps are composted at our facility and turned into rich, nutrient-dense soil over several weeks.

4

Gardens grow

That compost becomes the foundation of produce gardens planted in food desert neighborhoods across Pittsburgh.

5

Community eats

Fresh vegetables — tomatoes, peppers, greens, herbs — are available free of charge to residents in those neighborhoods.

Understanding the Problem

What is a food desert?

A food desert is an area where residents have limited or no access to affordable, nutritious food. In many Pittsburgh neighborhoods, the nearest grocery store is miles away and public transportation doesn't make it easy to get there.

⚠️ Pittsburgh Food Desert Facts

Several Pittsburgh neighborhoods — including parts of the Hill District, Homewood, Larimer, Hazelwood, and Knoxville — are classified as low-income, low-access food areas. Many residents rely on corner stores and fast food as their only nearby options for meals.

Living in a food desert is linked to higher rates of diabetes, heart disease, and obesity. Children in these areas are especially vulnerable to nutrition gaps that affect their development and school performance.

Pittsburgh Produce Planners works specifically in these neighborhoods, bringing gardens directly to the people who need them — no car required, no cost, no barriers.

Frequently Asked Questions

Questions & answers

Do I have to pay to take produce from the gardens?
No. All produce from PPP gardens is completely free. There is no registration, no income verification, no paperwork. Come and take what you need.
What scraps can I donate?
Vegetable peels, fruit scraps, coffee grounds and filters, eggshells, and tea bags. Please do not include meat, fish, dairy, oils, or cooked food in your donation bin.
How do I sign up for waste pickup?
Send us an email or call us (contact info below). We'll confirm your address is in a current pickup zone and drop off your free collection bin within a week.
Is my neighborhood going to get a garden?
We are actively expanding. If you live in a low-access food area and want to advocate for a garden on your block, reach out — community requests help us prioritize where we go next.
What is actually grown in the gardens?
We grow what's most useful and practical: tomatoes, peppers, zucchini, kale, lettuce, green beans, herbs like basil and cilantro, and seasonal vegetables suited to Pittsburgh's climate.
Is PPP a nonprofit?
Yes. Pittsburgh Produce Planners is a registered nonprofit organization in Pennsylvania. Donations are tax-deductible.
